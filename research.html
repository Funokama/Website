Research

My work to date sits at the intersection of behavioural economics, microeconomic theory, and quantitative methods. I am particularly interested in how psychologically grounded extensions of classical models can more realistically explain the behaviour of economic agents.

Research Areas

Behavioural Economics & Public Goods

Examining how reference-dependent preferences, warm-glow effects, and altruism reshape the classical free-rider problem in sequential contribution games.

Microeconomic Theory

Extensions of classical equilibrium models (e.g. Varian, 1994) with behavioural components and their welfare implications.

Econometrics & Data Analysis

Applied causal identification strategies (OLS, 2SLS, fixed effects) and data analysis with R, Python, and Stata.

Publications & Working Papers

Impact of reference-dependence on a sequential contributions game to public goods
Bachelor's thesis · University of Bern, Faculty of Business, Economic and Social Sciences · November 2024
Supervisor: Prof. Dr. Jean-Michel Benkert

This thesis examines the impact of reference-dependent preferences on a sequential contribution game of public goods, expanding on traditional models where free-riding behaviour is common. By incorporating reference dependency (where agents' wealth acts as a reference point) and warm-glow effects (intrinsic satisfaction from giving), the thesis analyses how these behavioural factors influence agents' contributions. Different forms of the warm-glow function (linear, concave, convex) are explored, and a disutility term is introduced to account for the discrepancy between contributions by different agents. The analysis demonstrates that these behavioural components significantly alter the equilibrium, reducing the tendency for free-riding and encouraging more equal contributions, resulting in higher overall provision than under traditional models.
